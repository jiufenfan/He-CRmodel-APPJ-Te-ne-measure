# ABSTRACT

The helium (He) non-thermal atmospheric pressure plasma jet (APPJ) source was configured, and the He spectra were measured by applying AC power to the source. A He collisional-radiative (CR) model was developed to investigate the He spectra obtained from the APPJ source. Different atmospheric pressure (AP) processes were evaluated, and the dominant processes among them that contribute to the He spectra were determined. The plasma parameters of the APPJ were determined using the He CR model, and the He spectra were successfully reproduced. The electron density was determined to be ${ \sim } 2 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ between the electrodes of the APPJ, and ${ \sim } 9 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 1 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ outside the quartz tube, for a frequency and bias of an AC voltage source of 5 kHz and $1 0 \mathrm { k V } _ { P \cdot P } ,$ , respectively. A parametric study was performed to explain the behavior of the electron temperature and electron density of APPJ.

# I. INTRODUCTION

The non-thermal atmospheric pressure plasma jet (APPJ) is a versatile plasma discharge source having a simple configuration and has considerable potential applications in biological surface treatment,1,2 plasma medicine,3,4 industrial application,5–8 and so on. Although various phenomena in APPJ have already been investigated by many research groups, its fundamental properties have not been fully understood.

Electrons play a key role in determining the physical and chemical properties of plasma. Thus, the electron density and temperature are important plasma parameters for understanding its fundamental properties. To measure these plasma parameters, various diagnostic methods have been developed, such as laser Thomson scattering (LTS),9–11 Langmuir probe,11–13 Stark broadening,14,15 and so on. Among these methods, the Langmuir probe method is difficult to apply to an APPJ because the plasma column is quite small compared to the probe size, and the probe can be easily damaged in atmospheric pressure (AP) plasmas. The LTS method requires a complicated and high performance system even though the method provides the exact electron temperature and density. The Stark broadening method is usually used for determining a high electron density above $1 0 ^ { 1 4 } { \mathrm { c m } } ^ { - 3 }$ because of Doppler broadening. To determine the electron density in the region of $1 0 ^ { 1 1 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ by the Stark broadening method, a saturated absorption spectroscopy system with high spectral resolution16,17 is required. The line intensity ratio (LIR) method combined with optical emission spectroscopy and the collision-radiative (CR) model has many advantages such as ease of spectrum measurement and simple configuration without affecting the plasma.18–21

Since the application of the LIR method combined with the CR model was applied to low temperature plasmas, the CR model has been steadily developed for helium (He) and argon plasmas. Recently, the accuracy of the He CR-model has been improved considerably and the use of the LIR method has become one of the alternatives for measuring the electron temperature and density of the low temperature He plasmas.22–26 The He CR-model for AP has been investigated independently of that for the low temperature He plasmas because of the various collision processes such as electron–atom, atom–atom, atom– molecule, and molecule–molecule. In addition, the atomic and molecular data for these processes in the case of He was insufficient. Since Alves summarized the atomic and molecular data for He,27 the He CR-model for AP has been developed in earnest. Belmonte et al. and Santos et al. have applied the He CR-model for AP to the resonance cavity and microwave capillary plasmas, respectively. Belmonte et al. have discussed the time evolution of the population density of the excited state and predicted the electron energy distribution function of AP He plasma.28 Santos et al. have improved the He CR-model for AP and determined the electron density using the Stark broadening method.29 Petrova et al. have composed a CR-model for He mixed with dry air, and they calculated the partial particle density in plasma as a function of He-to-air ratio.30 Claustre et al. have calculated the population density for AP He plasmas using their CR-model and compared their results with those of Claustre et al.31 However, to the best of our knowledge, diagnostics of the electron temperature and electron density using the He CR-model for AP have not been reported.

In this study, we have composed a He CR-model for APPJ in which the He CR-model for low temperature He plasmas has been extended to that for AP plasmas. We have summarized the elementary processes occurring at AP and evaluated the processes for investigating the dominant processes of He spectra. An APPJ source was configured and the He spectra obtained from the APPJ were measured for different values of flow rate, amplitude, and frequency of the AC bias. The electron temperature and density of APPJ were determined using the He CR model for AP, and the behavior of He spectra was discussed.

# II. THEORY

# A. Atomic and molecular processes in AP He plasmas

The CR-model is one of the population models from which the population density of excited state can be calculated. The accuracy of this CR-model depends on the accuracy of the atomic and molecular data. Since Ralchenko et al. reported the cross sections for the different electronic collision processes of neutral He atoms, the He CR-model for low temperature plasmas has been rapidly improved.32 Electronic collision processes such as the electron impact excitation/de-excitation, electron impact ionization, threebody recombination, radiative recombination and dielectronic recombination, and the radiative processes of the spontaneous emission and the radiation trapping effect (RTE) were involved in the He CR-model for low temperature He plasmas.18–26 In addition to these processes at low temperature, many other processes are involved in AP He plasmas, such as heavy-particle collision processes between the He atoms and molecules. Thus, these processes should be considered while calculating the He spectra at AP. All of processes involved in our CR-model are summarized in Table I.

In our model for AP He plasmas, the electron impact excitation/ de-excitation processes for levels having the principal quantum number (n) of up to 20 were considered, and the same atomic data as that used in Goto’s CR-model19 were adopted for the electronic collisional process and spontaneous radiative processes (reactions 1–5 in Table I) The reaction processes of 6–25 in Table I were additionally considered for developing the He CR model for AP plasmas. Most processes have been reported and discussed by Belmonte and Santos in detail. 28,29 We have adopted their atomic and molecular data except for processes 1–6 and 20 in Table I.

At AP, most processes involve the ground state, metastable states, and the excited state with the n ¼ 2 in the case of the He atom. Kajita et al. have evaluated the impact of the change in population density on the different energy states of the He atom.40 Their result showed that the change in population density of the n ¼ 2 state primarily influenced the excited states having the same principal quantum number and hardly influenced the excited states with n ¼ 3 and n ¼ 4.

Most He spectra in the visible range involve the excited states of n ¼ 3 and n ¼ 4. The population densities of the excited states influence the excited state having the same principal quantum number. This is because the energy difference in this case is less than that between excited states having different principal quantum numbers. Among AP processes, the angular quantum number exchange, i.e., the so-called l-exchange reaction, associative ionization (AI), and dissociative recombination (DR) involve excited states having n ¼ 3 and n ¼ 4. Thus, these processes are important for investigating the He spectra in AP He plasmas. The l-exchange reactions are typically heavy particle interactions, and the details of the rate coefficients have been explained by Belmonte et al.28 and Santos et al.29 In addition, the process is reversible and the inverse rate coefficient was obtained by the principle of detailed balance. The l-exchange reaction is a populating as well as depopulating process of excited states and enables the redistribution of the population of any excited state to other excited states having the same principal quantum number.

The AI process is one of the heavy particle collision processes similar to the l-exchange reaction. Its reactants are the same as those of l-exchange reaction. However, its products are Heþ and an electron. Thus, AI acts as a depopulating process for the excited state in a neutral He atom. Recent data on the AI processes have been suggested by Belmonte et al.28 and Santos et al.29 In their work, the gas temperature was 2450 K. However, the gas temperature of an APPJ is known to be 300–310 K, and it is difficult to apply their ionization rate coefficient to our work. Thus, we calculated the AI rate coefficients for the triplet states having n ¼ 3 and n ¼ 4 using the cross section provided by Cohen41 to determine the exact rate coefficients when the gas temperature was 310 K. The rate coefficient of the 31S state was assumed to be 10 times larger than that of the 33S state because the cross section of the former at 300 K was expected to be 10 times larger than that of the latter.28 The rate coefficients of the 31P and 31D states were calculated by convoluting the cross sections reported by Dubreuil and Catherinot42 with the Maxwellian energy distribution function. The other rate coefficients were calculated using the relation of the temperature dependency suggested by Belmonte and Santos. The calculated rate coefficients are summarized in Table II.

The DR process (reaction 20 in Table I) is an important recombination process of molecular He ions with electrons in AP plasmas and is the inverse process of AI. The process is a populating process for excited states of He atoms and its importance has been discussed by Bibinov et al.43 and Ivanov et al.44 Thus, it is one of the important processes for investigating the He spectra obtained at AP. However, in the case of the He atom, most of the reported rate coefficients were for the ground state and the excited state with n ¼ 2. Thus, the rate coefficients for the DR process were calculated by integrating the cross sections suggested by Royal over the Maxwellian electron energy distribution function.39 In our work, we have evaluated the influence of the l-exchange reaction, the AI process, and the DR process using the He CR-model for AP.

# III. EXPERIMENT

Figure 2 shows the schematic diagram of our APPJ. The APPJ was configured using a quartz tube having inner and outer diameters of 4 mm and 6 mm, respectively, and a length of 200 mm. The first electrode made up of copper was installed inside the quartz tube. A sinusoidal waveform signal was produced using a function generator (Keysight, 33500B), and the signal was transferred to an AC amplifier (Trek, 10/10B-HS), which amplified the input signal by 1000 times. The amplitude and frequency of the AC bias were controlled by the function generator. The amplified AC bias with a frequency of 3–9 kHz was applied to the first electrode to produce APPJ. The second electrode, made up of copper and having a width of 10 mm and thickness of 1 mm, was wrapped tightly around the quartz tube and the electrode was grounded. The distance between two electrodes was 20 mm, and the second electrode was placed 20 mm away from the end of the quartz tube. To discharge He APPJ, high purity He gas (99:999%) was injected from a distance of 50 mm from the other end of the quartz tube. The He gas flow was controlled by the mass-flow rate-controller in the range of 1–5 standard liter per minute (SLM).

# IV. RESULT AND DISCUSSION

Figure 3 shows the population densities of the 31S; 33S; 31D, and 33D excited states calculated using Eq. (2) as a function of the electron density. The black dashed line and solid line represent the population densities without and with the AP processes, respectively. The orange line represents the population density including all AP processes except AI, DR, and l-exchange reactions. The other colored lines represent the population densities when AI (green), DR (blue), and l-exchange reactions (red) were additionally incorporated in the calculations represented by the orange line. When the AP processes without the above-mentioned three processes were included, the population densities in Figs. 3(a)–3(d) were observed to change by less than 2% in $N _ { e } = 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { { c m } ^ { - ; } }$ 3 and the change of population density could be neglected. Most AP processes excluding the abovementioned three processes involve the ground state and the n ¼ 2 state. However, the upper state of the visible transition of He involves the excited states of n ¼ 3 and 4. It is difficult for the change in the population density of any excited state to affect the higher excited state. This phenomenon was reported by Kajita and Ohno,40 and in their report, an increase in the population density of the 21P state did not influence the excited states having different principal quantum numbers. Thus, the AP processes without the above three processes could not influence the He spectra in the visible range.

When the DR process was additionally included in the calculations represented by the orange line, the population density of the 33D state changed by only a small amount of 0:1 % at $N _ { e } = 1 \stackrel { \cdot } { \times } 1 0 ^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ . When the electron density is higher than $1 0 ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 } ,$ the DR process clearly influences the population density of the excited state. However, for an electron density of less than $1 0 ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ , this process did not affect the excited states and can, thus, be neglected. For the electron density less than $1 0 ^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ , the densities of the electron and molecular helium ion are much smaller than that of the neutral He atoms at AP. As Bibinov and Ivanov have discussed,43,44 the rate coefficient of the DR in the case of the 33D state is $3 . 7 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 0 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { 3 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ , and this value is larger than the rate coefficient of AI corresponding to the same state. The population density of the excited state would increase by the DR processes in AP He plasmas. However, for a gas density larger than $\dot { 1 } 0 ^ { 1 9 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ in AP plasmas, the electron density of the APPJ was found to be $1 0 ^ { 1 1 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 } - 1 0 ^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ , and the density of Heþ should be less than the electron density. Thus, the population density of the excited state was mainly determined by the transition from the ground and metastable states, and the DR process was expected to influence the population density of the excited states of He in AP plasmas only slightly when the electron density is less than $1 0 ^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ .

To understand the plasma characteristics of the APPJ, we applied the He CR-model for $\mathrm { A P }$ [expressed by Eq. (2)] to the APPJ spectra. The plasma parameters, namely, the electron density and temperature, were determined using the He CR-model combined with the LIR method. The electron density and temperature were determined as the values minimizing the error function as follows:21–26

$$
f _ {e r r} (T _ {e}, N _ {e}, N (2 ^ {1} \mathrm{S}), N (2 ^ {3} \mathrm{S}), K (3 ^ {1} \mathrm{P})) = \sqrt {\sum \left(\frac {L _ {\exp} - L _ {C R M}}{L _ {C R M}}\right) ^ {2}}, \tag {4}
$$

where $L _ { e x p }$ and $L _ { C R M }$ are the LIRs experimentally measured and calculated, respectively, using the He CR-model. Three LIRs of 587.6m $( 3 ^ { 3 } D - 2 ^ { \widehat { 3 } } P ) / 7 2 8 . 1$ 1 nm $\bar { ( 3 ^ { 1 } S } - 2 ^ { 1 } P )$ , 667.8 nm $( 3 ^ { 1 } D - 2 ^ { 1 } P ) / 7 2 8 . 1$ nm, and 728.1 nm/706.5 nm $( 3 ^ { 3 } S - 2 ^ { 3 } P )$ were adopted as the $L _ { e x p }$ and $L _ { C R M } .$ In low temperature plasmas, 4–6 LIRs were adopted to increase the accuracy of the LIR method. However, only four spectra of the APPJ were measured in our experiment, and we adopted 3 LIRs. Only the RTE for the $3 ^ { 1 } P$ state was considered in this study because RTE depends on the population density of the $n ^ { \mathrm { 1 p } }$ states as well as the density of the ground state.21 The spectral lines involved in $n = 4$ state of He could not be measured. The averaged values from 10 sets of the determined parameters having $f _ { e r r } <$ < 0.05 are shown in Figs. 6–8.

# V. CONCLUSION

We have developed a He CR-model for AP plasmas to diagnose electron temperature and electron density of an APPJ. Our developed model was extended from the formulation I of Goto’s He CR-model that was developed for understanding low temperature He plasmas. The AP processes (6–25 in Table I) were additionally included in the CR-model and the influence of AI, l-exchange reaction, and the DR process on the population density of the excited states was evaluated. AI was the most dominant process among AP processes. A simple expression of the He CR-model for AP plasmas was suggested.

An APPJ source was configured using a quartz tube and AC power. Plasma radiation at the position between two electrodes, between the second electrode and the end of quartz tube, and outside the quartz tube, was analyzed using the He CR-model for AP processes. Various spectra of He, $\mathrm { H e } _ { 2 } , \mathrm { N } _ { 2 } , \mathrm { N } _ { 2 } ^ { + }$ , and OH radical were measured. Among the He spectral lines observed in the visible range, those corresponding to the excited state with n ¼ 3 were only measured. The spectra of the OH radical and He in section A, the spectra of Nþ and He in section B, and the spectra of N2; Nþ , and He in section C were observed to be highly intense. The electron temperature and density in each section were determined using our He CR-model for the AP process. When the AC frequency and bias were changed in all sections, the electron temperature remained almost constant, whereas the electron density in section A increased up to $8 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ . The electron density was clearly larger in section A as compared to that in sections B and C. The electron densities in sections B and C did not change and remained constant when the AC frequency and bias were changed. The electron density in section C was always larger than that in section B. However, when He gas flow was increased, the electron density in section C decreased significantly $\mathrm { t o } \sim 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 1 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ .

Thus, the electron density in section A in our quartz tube was observed to be clearly dependent on the AC bias and frequency. However, the density in section C, which is useful for various application, was sensitively dependent on the He gas flow rather than the AC bias and frequency.
